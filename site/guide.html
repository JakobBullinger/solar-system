Visitor’s guide

The Solar System — a live orrery

An interactive solar system where nothing is animated by hand: every planet, moon, comet and spacecraft position is computed live from real orbital mechanics — JPL orbital elements, an n-body integrator, a full lunar theory. It runs in your browser as a single file, no network, no account. This page is the written map; every link below drops you straight into a specific moment of the app.

The 10-minute path

If you do only six things, do these — in order.

  1. ~3 minTake the grand tour

    It offers itself on a first visit (or ✦ Explore → Grand tour): twelve stops from the Sun to the edge of the map, including everything below.

  2. ~1 minClick a planet, then drag time

    Click any world for its dossier with live telemetry; drag the speed slider at the bottom from real time up to a year per second and watch the clockwork run. space pauses; the address bar always holds a shareable link to exactly what you see.

  3. ~1 minWatch the 2026 total eclipse

    Use the eclipse link below (or ✦ Explore → Events): the Moon’s umbra — real geometry, real timing — sweeps across the Atlantic into Spain.

  4. ~2 minBreak the solar system

    ✦ Explore → Sandbox, then a what-if scenario: a second Jupiter, a companion star, a rogue star, or deflecting an asteroid. The planets honestly leave their rails — and one click restores reality.

  5. ~2 minFly a mission

    ✦ Explore → Missions: drag a departure burn off Earth, watch the preview arc chase a moving target, and try to make par. Winning runs become challenge links you can send to a friend.

  6. ~1 minScroll past the edges

    Zoom all the way out and keep scrolling: the map hands over to a powers-of-ten zoom out to the Local Group. Zoom into Earth and keep going: a kilometre-scale regime with 4,408 Starlink satellites, the ISS and the geostationary ring. Esc or scroll back always comes home.

Honesty notes: distances on the map are compressed (the true solar system is almost entirely empty space — at true scale Earth would sit 1.6 km from an on-screen Sun); planet sizes are enhanced unless True size is on; the eclipse umbra’s size on the globe is schematic while its timing and track are real; Starlink positions are a synthetic catalog with the real shell structure. Everything else — orbits, dates, Δv numbers, eclipse instants — is computed, tested against published values, and meant to be trusted. Built with Claude.
